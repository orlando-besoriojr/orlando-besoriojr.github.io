Annotated Bibliography

A Taxonomy of Knowledge Gaps for Wikimedia Projects (Second Draft).

In this paper, the Wikimedia Foundation gives an open view on the state of knowledge gaps in their database, mainly divided into three categories, Content, Contributors, and Readers. Disenfranchisement, the complexity of race, along with the need for culture/region-specific context have led to an overall under-representation of POC on Wikidata.

Redi, Miriam, et al. “A Taxonomy of Knowledge Gaps for Wikimedia Projects (Second Draft).” Arxiv, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2008.12314.pdf.

Analyzing Race and Citizenship Bias in Wikidata

“Analyzing Race and Citizenship Bias in Wikidata” is a shorter length and more statistically dense paper that lays out what can be found on the site itself through queries and surveys. The research team made a hypothesis that the edits made about STEM representation for scientists was heavily skewed towards white men from North America and Europe.

Z. Shaik, F. Ilievski and F. Morstatter, "Analyzing Race and Citizenship Bias in Wikidata," 2021 IEEE 18th International Conference on Mobile Ad Hoc and Smart Systems (MASS), 2021, pp. 665-666, doi: https://doi.org/10.1109/MASS52906.2021.00099.

RACE AND ONLINE CONTENT CREATION

This paper by Teresa Correa and Sun Ho Jeong delves into how different races approach online content creation and their many nuances. One of my largest takeaways are the authors' observations of “three main principles regarding participation [organizing discourse] among minority groups on the internet.” These are Connecting, Enacting the self, and Struggling. Niche communities, self-expression, uncontrolled content, and many more make up the discourse people of color tend to participate in.

Correa, Teresa, and Sun Ho Jeong. “RACE AND ONLINE CONTENT CREATION.” Information, Communication & Society, vol. 14, no. 5, Routledge, 2011, pp. 638–59, https://doi.org/10.1080/1369118X.2010.514355.

Understanding Diversity through Wikipedia and Wikidata

Will Kent, a program manager for multiple Wikimedia projects, acknowledges how Wikipedia and Wikidata operate with systemic bias. He mentions that the query tool and database of Wikidata could act like a magnifier to “identify areas of improvement.” In the article, Kent calls for more communities to participate and take advantage of Wikimedia's openness to improve representation.

Kent, Will. “Understanding Diversity through Wikipedia and Wikidata.” Wiki Education, 6 Jan. 2022, https://wikiedu.org/blog/2022/01/05/understanding-diversity-through-wikipedia-and-wikidata/.

U.S. College Students' Internet Use: Race, Gender and Digital Divides.

This paper covers how race correlates to internet usage, its causes, and the overarching digital divide created by it. The paper concludes that the digital divide is caused by socioeconomic inequality affecting people of colors’ ability to access the internet. The purpose for internet usage between different races, however, remained the same as they were all able to communicate, access information and entertainment at relatively the same level.

Jones, Steve, et al. “U.S. College Students' Internet Use: Race, Gender and Digital Divides.” Journal of Computer-Mediated Communication, vol. 14, no. 2, Blackwell Publishing Ltd, 2009, pp. 244–64, https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1083-6101.2009.01439.x.

"Who do you think you are?": Race, Representation, and Cultural Rhetorics in Online Spaces

Andre Brock's paper primarily focuses on the state of Black identity compared from the real world to the online space. They define the internet as a “third space” that provides a space for people to break free from the confines of the real world and be who they want to be. The discursive nature of the internet is an important tool used to empower/present racial identities when in the real world, systemic bias and racism cloud incredibly important perspectives. “Third places serve a regenerative function for the people who frequent their environs; where they can reconstitute themselves as 'people' apart from their kinship and work networks.”

Brock, A., (2009) “"Who do you think you are?": Race, Representation, and Cultural Rhetorics in Online Spaces”, Poroi 6(1), p.15-35. doi: https://doi.org/10.13008/2151-2957.1013

Internet Usage: A Within Race Analysis

This paper complements the “U.S. College Students' Internet Use: Race, Gender and Digital Divides” in that it explores digital divide and differential use between races. The authors conclude that differential use is shrinking, but the divides are still prevalent. One thing to note is that this study only did research based white and non-white.

Banerjee, Sarbani, and Amitra Hodge. “Internet Usage: A Within Race Analysis.” Race, Gender & Class, vol. 14, no. 3/4, Jean Ait Belkhir, Race, Gender & Class Journal, 2007, pp. 228–46, http://www.jstor.org/stable/41675301.

Measuring the Digital Divide in the United States: Race, Income, and Personal Computer Ownership

Another piece that relates to “U.S. College Students' Internet Use: Race, Gender and Digital Divides,” this article mentions differential usage/ownership and economic inequality as factors causing the digital divide.

Chakraborty, Jayajit, and M. Martin Bosman. “Measuring the Digital Divide in the United States: Race, Income, and Personal Computer Ownership.” The Professional Geographer, vol. 57, no. 3, Taylor & Francis Group, 2005, pp. 395-410, https://doi.org/10.1111/j.0033-0124.2005.00486.x.

Race and Racism in Internet Studies: A Review and Critique

One of the biggest takeaways from this article I have found is Jessie Daniels referring to the inherent “whiteness” of the internet. A long-term societal “white racial frame” had been established allowing for a continuation of unbalanced power structures to replicate itself from the real world on to the internet. As Daniels says, this leaves “the burden of noticing race” up to researchers who are people of color. This denial of racism and the belief that we are living in a color-blind web is more often than not reinforcing discrimination and intolerance.

Daniels, Jessie. “Race and Racism in Internet Studies: A Review and Critique.” New Media & Society, vol. 15, no. 5, Aug. 2013, pp. 695–719, doi:10.1177/1461444812462849.

The Spectacle of the Other

This paper has become the central topic of my project. In this article, Stuart Hall analyzes binary oppositions, the stereotypes it creates, how it maintains social and symbolic order, and three suggestions to reverse the process.

Hall, Stuart. "The spectacle of the other." Representation: Cultural representations and signifying practices 7 (1997).
